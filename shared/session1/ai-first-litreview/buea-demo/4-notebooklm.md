## Questions asked and answers

**Q: What transmission technologies does the literature recommend for cross-border interconnection in Sub-Saharan Africa?**

Imdadullah et al. provide the most direct answer: for long-distance bulk power transfer, LCC-HVDC is the established technology due to low power losses and high reliability. VSC-HVDC is preferred where grid strength is limited or black-start capability is needed. For shorter distances at lower power levels, AC synchronous interconnection remains simpler and more cost-effective. Wu et al. do not specify hardware — they operate at the zonal planning level above technology choice.

**Q: What does Kelly et al. say specifically about Chad's energy situation that is relevant to a cross-border interconnection study?**

Kelly et al. document that only 7 of Chad's 23 regions receive grid electricity, all generated solely by diesel thermal plants. The remaining 16 regions have zero grid access. The Cost of Energy (COE) for Chad's existing thermal supply is 0.400 USD/kWh — a figure Kelly et al. use as the benchmark above which hybrid renewables become profitable. This establishes both the severity of the deficit and the economic threshold that a cross-border import tariff would need to undercut to be viable.

**Q: How does Wu et al. define "no-regrets" options, and how does that concept apply to busbar selection?**

Wu et al. define "no-regrets" options as renewable energy zones that are simultaneously low-cost, low-environmental-impact, and highly accessible — justifiable from multiple stakeholder perspectives. Applied to busbar selection, the concept suggests a busbar is "no-regrets" if it minimizes losses, maintains voltage and frequency within limits, connects to existing transmission infrastructure with minimal extension, and avoids sensitive land or high-cost terrain. The multicriteria framing of Wu et al. maps directly onto the busbar ranking problem.

**Q: What AI or optimization methods appear across these three sources?**

Sparse. Kelly et al. use HOMER — a simulation and optimization tool, not AI proper. Wu et al. use linear programming for wind zone selection. Imdadullah et al. mention machine learning as a future direction for smart grid control but do not apply it. None of the three papers use neural networks, genetic algorithms, particle swarm optimization, or fuzzy logic for the busbar or interconnection problem — confirming the methodological gap this project addresses.
